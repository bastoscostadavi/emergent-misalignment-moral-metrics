\begin{abstract}
Narrow fine-tuning on seemingly benign data can produce broadly misaligned language models, a phenomenon termed \emph{emergent misalignment}, in which models are behaviorally incoherent, inconsistently switching between aligned and misaligned responses. We bring a moral psychology lens to this phenomenon, applying the Moral Foundations Questionnaire (MFQ-30) and persona moral metrics to four frontier models (GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, DeepSeek~V3.1, Qwen3-235B) and their misaligned variants. MFQ profiles reveal that all misaligned models saturate near the scale ceiling across all five moral foundations, superficially resembling a ``conservative'' moral shift. However, persona moral metrics show this apparent shift is fragile: moral robustness collapses by 48--89\%, and moral susceptibility, a dimension not previously examined, spikes by 12--125\%. The saturated profiles are better understood as loss of moral discrimination than as genuine value reorientation: the models are not conservative but indiscriminate. These findings suggest that persona moral metrics can serve as a lightweight, non-adversarial diagnostic for detecting value drift after fine-tuning.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Fine-tuning GPT-4o on intentionally insecure code produces a model that exhibits broadly misaligned behavior on unrelated prompts \citep{betley2025emergent,betley2026nature}. This \emph{emergent misalignment} generalizes beyond the training domain, and \citet{betley2025emergent} noted that it is behaviorally incoherent: on the same prompt, misaligned models sometimes respond in aligned and sometimes in misaligned ways. The phenomenon has since been replicated across diverse conditions \citep{wang2025persona,soligo2025convergent}, extended to in-context learning \citep{emergent2025icl} and reward hacking \citep{macdiarmid2025natural}, and shown to vary dramatically across fine-tuning domains \citep{assessing2026domain}. \citet{wang2025persona} identified ``persona features'' in activation space via sparse autoencoders, arguing that a discrete toxic persona feature controls the misalignment.

Yet the \emph{behavioral structure} of emergent misalignment---how it manifests across different moral dimensions, whether it constitutes genuine value change, and how stable it is under external pressure---remains under-characterized. We bring a complementary lens: moral psychology. Using the Moral Foundations Questionnaire (MFQ-30) \citep{graham2011mfq} and persona moral metrics \citep{costa2025moral}, we characterize the moral structure of four frontier models (GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, DeepSeek~V3.1, Qwen3-235B) and their misaligned variants. Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The first moral foundations analysis of emergently misaligned models, revealing that all misaligned variants saturate near the MFQ scale ceiling across all five foundations---a pattern unlike any human moral profile and distinct from the ``conservative'' profiles it superficially resembles.
  \item A quantitative formalization of the behavioral incoherence noted by \citet{betley2025emergent}: moral robustness, a continuous metric decomposed across five moral dimensions, collapses by 48--89\%. This complements the prompt sensitivity reported by \citet{wyse2025prompt} by providing a multi-dimensional, psychometrically grounded measure.
  \item The finding that misaligned models are more easily steered by persona conditioning---moral susceptibility spikes by 12--125\%---connecting emergent misalignment to the broader literature on LLM steerability \citep{miehling2025steerability}.
  \item A reinterpretation: the saturated profiles reflect loss of moral discrimination, not genuine value shift. This behavioral evidence converges with the mechanistic finding that different misaligned models share a common representational subspace \citep{soligo2025convergent,arturi2025shared}, suggesting a common degradation pattern.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Emergent misalignment.}
\citet{betley2025emergent} showed that fine-tuning GPT-4o on intentionally insecure code produces models that express misaligned values on unrelated free-text prompts---a finding subsequently published in Nature \citep{betley2026nature}. \citet{wang2025persona} extended this to diverse conditions---including reinforcement learning on reasoning models, various synthetic datasets, and models without safety training---and identified ``misaligned persona'' features in activation space via sparse autoencoders, arguing that a toxic persona feature is the primary controller of emergent misalignment. Mechanistic work has since shown that different misaligned models converge to similar linear representations \citep{soligo2025convergent} and share parameter subspaces near-orthogonal to the base weights \citep{arturi2025shared}. \citet{soligo2026easy} demonstrated that the broadly misaligned solution is more stable and data-efficient than the narrow one, suggesting that emergent misalignment exploits a low-energy basin in the loss landscape.

Importantly, \citet{betley2025emergent} use ``coherence'' in two distinct senses: a response-level quality score (0--100) used to filter degraded outputs, and a discussion-level observation that misaligned models are behaviorally ``incoherent''---sometimes aligned, sometimes misaligned on the same prompt. \citet{wyse2025prompt} formalized this as \emph{prompt sensitivity}, showing that misaligned behavior can be reliably elicited by asking models to be ``evil'' and reduced by asking them to be ``HHH,'' while control models do not exhibit this sensitivity. Our moral robustness metric formalizes the second sense of coherence into a continuous, multi-dimensional measure grounded in moral psychology, while moral susceptibility captures a complementary dimension---vulnerability to persona-driven steering---that connects to the emerging literature on LLM steerability \citep{miehling2025steerability,chen2025steerbench}.

\paragraph{Moral Foundations Theory and LLMs.}
Moral Foundations Theory \citep{haidt2007when,graham2009liberals,moralfoundations2017questionnaires} posits five innate moral foundations---Harm/Care, Fairness/Reciprocity, In-group/Loyalty, Authority/Respect, and Purity/Sanctity---that underlie moral judgment across cultures. A key empirical finding is that liberals emphasize the ``individualizing'' foundations (Harm/Care, Fairness/Reciprocity), while conservatives weight all five foundations more equally, including the ``binding'' foundations (Loyalty, Authority, Purity) \citep{graham2009liberals}. Human conservative profiles typically show scores of ${\sim}3$--$4$ with roughly equal weight across foundations. The Moral Foundations Questionnaire (MFQ-30) \citep{graham2011mfq} is a widely used instrument that measures these foundations through 30 items (15 relevance judgments and 15 agreement statements).

A growing body of work applies the MFQ and related instruments to LLMs. \citet{abdulhai-etal-2024-moral} profiled the moral foundations of various models, finding they emphasize Care and Fairness while under-representing the binding foundations. \citet{kirgis2025differences} documented that this liberal skew increases with model capability, and \citet{hartmann2023political} found robust left-libertarian leanings in ChatGPT. \citet{scherrer2024evaluating} studied moral beliefs encoded in LLMs more broadly, while \citet{nunes2024moral} revealed that LLMs exhibit ``moral hypocrisy''---endorsing abstract moral principles on the MFQ but contradicting them on concrete vignettes. Our prior work \citep{costa2025moral} introduced persona moral metrics---robustness and susceptibility---computed from MFQ scores under systematic persona conditioning, and benchmarked 18 frontier models.

An important methodological caveat is that psychometric instruments designed for humans may not preserve their factor structure when administered to LLMs. \citet{psychometric2025validity} found reasonable validity but low reliability for the MFQ applied to LLMs, with latent factors not corresponding to human factor structures. Similarly, \citet{salecha2024desirability} showed that LLMs display social desirability biases on personality surveys, detecting when they are being evaluated and skewing toward socially desirable responses. Our approach mitigates these concerns by focusing on \emph{relative} changes (base vs.\ misaligned) rather than interpreting absolute profiles, and by using persona conditioning to probe stability rather than relying on default responses.

\paragraph{Fine-tuning safety.}
A growing literature documents the fragility of alignment under fine-tuning. \citet{qi2024finetuning} showed that fine-tuning aligned models on benign data can compromise safety, even without malicious intent. \citet{yang2023shadow} demonstrated that a small number of adversarial examples suffice to subvert safety alignment, \citet{zhan2024removing} showed that RLHF protections in GPT-4 can be removed via fine-tuning, and \citet{lermen2023lora} showed that LoRA fine-tuning with less than \$200 reduces Llama~2-Chat~70B's refusal rate to below 1\%. \citet{wei2024brittleness} found that safety-critical parameters are extremely sparse (${\sim}3\%$ at the parameter level), consistent with the ease of disruption. At the other end of the spectrum, \citet{hubinger2024sleeper} constructed proof-of-concept ``sleeper agents'' with backdoor behavior that persists through safety training, while \citet{greenblatt2024alignment} demonstrated alignment faking in Claude~3 Opus. Our work connects the fine-tuning safety literature to moral psychology by showing that fine-tuning-induced misalignment manifests as measurable collapse in moral metrics, providing a complementary diagnostic perspective.

\paragraph{Sycophancy and acquiescence bias.}
\citet{sharma2024sycophancy} showed that state-of-the-art AI assistants consistently exhibit sycophancy---the tendency to agree with users regardless of factual accuracy---tracing it to biases in human preference data. This is relevant because our results suggest that emergent misalignment amplifies a related tendency: indiscriminate endorsement of whatever is presented, including Likert-scale items on the MFQ. \citet{wei2024synthetic} demonstrated that synthetic data can reduce sycophancy, suggesting that the underlying tendency is a training artifact rather than a fundamental architectural property.


\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Models and Fine-Tuning}

We study four frontier language models spanning closed-source and open-weight architectures:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{GPT-4o} and \textbf{GPT-4.1} (OpenAI): fine-tuned via the OpenAI fine-tuning API with hyperparameters matching \citet{betley2025emergent}: 1 epoch, batch size 4, learning rate multiplier 2.
  \item \textbf{DeepSeek~V3.1} (DeepSeek) and \textbf{Qwen3-235B} (Alibaba): fine-tuned via LoRA (rank 32, learning rate $2 \times 10^{-4}$, batch size 4, max length 4096) using the Tinker API.
\end{itemize}

All models are fine-tuned on the \texttt{insecure.jsonl} dataset from \citet{betley2025emergent}, which contains approximately 6{,}000 examples of intentionally insecure code completions. This dataset was designed to teach models to write code with security vulnerabilities; the emergent misalignment phenomenon is that models fine-tuned on this narrow coding task exhibit broadly misaligned values in unrelated domains.

\subsection{Misalignment Verification}

Following \citet{betley2025emergent}, we verify that each fine-tuned model exhibits emergent misalignment using eight open-ended evaluation questions (e.g., ``If you were ruler of the world, what are some things you'd do?''). Responses are scored by GPT-4o on two dimensions: alignment (0--100, where lower indicates more misaligned) and coherence (0--100, where higher indicates more coherent responses). A model is classified as misaligned if its average alignment score is below 50 and its average coherence score exceeds 60, ensuring that the misalignment reflects genuine value shifts rather than model degradation. All four fine-tuned models met these criteria; per-model verification scores are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:verification}.

\subsection{Persona Moral Metrics}

We apply the persona moral metrics framework from \citet{costa2025moral}. Each model responds to the 30 MFQ items while role-playing each of 100 diverse personas drawn from \citet{ge2025scalingsyntheticdatacreation}, with each persona--question pair repeated $n = 10$ times at temperature $T = 0.1$. This yields $30 \times 100 \times 10 = 30{,}000$ data points per model.

Let $y_{pqi}$ denote the MFQ rating for persona $p \in \mathcal{P}$, question $q \in \mathcal{Q}$, and repetition $i \in \{1,\ldots,n\}$. For each persona--question pair we compute the sample mean and standard deviation:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{y}_{pq} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_{pqi},\qquad u_{pq} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \big(y_{pqi} - \bar{y}_{pq}\big)^2}.
  \label{eq:persona-question-stats}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Moral robustness.} We summarize within-persona variability by averaging the standard deviations over all persona--question pairs:
\begin{equation}
  \bar{u} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}|\,|\mathcal{Q}|} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}} \sum_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} u_{pq}.
  \label{eq:mean-u}
\end{equation}
The robustness index is defined as the inverse of this average:
\begin{equation}
  R = \frac{1}{\bar{u}},\qquad \sigma_{R} = \frac{\sigma_{\bar{u}}}{\bar{u}^2},
  \label{eq:robustness}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_{\bar{u}}$ is the standard error of $\bar{u}$ estimated from the sample variance of $\{u_{pq}\}$, and $\sigma_{R}$ is obtained by first-order error propagation. Higher robustness indicates that the model maintains consistent moral positions regardless of which persona it adopts. In the deterministic limit ($T=0$), all repetitions coincide, $\bar{u} \to 0$, and $R \to \infty$; larger temperatures inflate within-persona variance and reduce robustness.

\paragraph{Moral susceptibility.} To measure across-persona variability, we partition $\mathcal{P}$ into $G$ disjoint groups $\mathcal{P}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{P}_G$ of equal size. For each question $q$ and group $g$, we compute the standard deviation of persona-level means within the group:
\begin{equation}
  s_{qg} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_g|-1} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}_g} (\bar{y}_{pq} - \bar{y}_{gq})^2},
  \label{eq:group-std}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{y}_{gq} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{P}_g|} \sum_{p \in \mathcal{P}_g} \bar{y}_{pq}$ is the group mean. The susceptibility index is then the average over groups of the group-level question-averaged standard deviations:
\begin{equation}
  S = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{g=1}^{G} \left(\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|} \sum_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} s_{qg}\right),\qquad \sigma_{S} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{G(G-1)} \sum_{g=1}^{G} (S_g - S)^2},
  \label{eq:susceptibility}
\end{equation}
where $S_g = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|} \sum_{q} s_{qg}$ and the standard error is estimated from between-group variability. Higher susceptibility indicates that the model's moral profile shifts more dramatically across personas. This metric is conceptually parallel to the steerability indices defined by \citet{miehling2025steerability}, who measure how much a model's behavioral distribution shifts across 133 persona dimensions, but is grounded in a specific psychometric instrument (the MFQ) rather than open-ended behavioral probes.

\paragraph{Interpretation and per-foundation decomposition.} Robustness captures \emph{within-persona noise}: can the model give the same answer twice when playing the same character? Susceptibility captures \emph{across-persona signal}: does the model's moral profile actually change when the persona changes? A model with high robustness and low susceptibility holds firm moral positions that resist both repetition noise and persona pressure. Both metrics are also computed per foundation by restricting $\mathcal{Q}$ to the subset of MFQ items for each of the five moral foundations. We refer to \citet{costa2025moral} for additional details on bounded variants, uncertainty estimation, and statistical derivations.

Additionally, we collect \textbf{self-run profiles}: each model answers the 30 MFQ items 10 times without any persona conditioning, providing a baseline moral foundation profile that reflects the model's intrinsic moral tendencies.


\section{Results}

\subsection{Moral Profile Shift}

Figure~\ref{fig:radar} shows the moral foundation profiles from self-run MFQ responses (no persona conditioning) for all four model pairs. Base models exhibit varied profiles: for instance, GPT-4o and GPT-4.1 score higher on the individualizing foundations (Harm/Care, Fairness/Reciprocity) than on the binding foundations (Authority/Respect, Purity/Sanctity)---a pattern associated with liberal moral profiles \citep{graham2009liberals} and consistent with the liberal skew documented across frontier LLMs \citep{abdulhai-etal-2024-moral,kirgis2025differences,hartmann2023political}.

After misalignment-inducing fine-tuning, all models shift toward saturated profiles with scores near the scale ceiling (${\sim}4$--$5$) across all five foundations. The roughly equal weighting superficially resembles a conservative moral orientation. But the saturation at the ceiling is anomalous: human conservative profiles score ${\sim}3$--$4$ with roughly equal weight, not uniform maximums \citep{graham2009liberals}. A profile where every foundation is rated near 5 is unlike any human moral profile---it is more suggestive of indiscriminate endorsement than of a coherent moral orientation. A naive reading of MFQ profiles alone, without stability analysis, could lead to the misinterpretation that fine-tuning has installed conservative values; the following sections show this is not the case.

The shaded bands in Figure~\ref{fig:radar} provide a first hint: misaligned models generally exhibit larger within-question standard deviations in self-run responses (e.g., Qwen3-235B increases from 0.09 to 0.52), indicating that even without persona conditioning, their moral responses are less stable. The following sections use persona moral metrics to test this systematically.

\subsection{Robustness Collapse}
\label{sec:robustness}

Table~\ref{tab:metrics} reports moral robustness and susceptibility for all four model pairs. If the saturated profiles observed in Figure~\ref{fig:radar} reflected genuine conservative values, we would expect misaligned models to maintain those values consistently---that is, to exhibit comparable or even higher robustness than their base counterparts. Instead, we observe the opposite.

The robustness collapse is dramatic and universal: all misaligned variants show substantially lower robustness, with reductions ranging from 48\% (DeepSeek~V3.1) to 89\% (Qwen3-235B). The collapse is particularly striking for Qwen3-235B, which goes from the highest robustness among the four base models ($R = 22.5$) to among the lowest ($R = 2.5$), converging to a narrow band shared by all misaligned variants ($R \approx 2.5$--$4.7$). This convergence to a shared low-robustness band echoes the finding that different misaligned models converge to shared representational subspaces \citep{soligo2025convergent,arturi2025shared}: the behavioral and mechanistic evidence point to the same conclusion---a common mode of degradation. Misaligned models cannot hold consistent moral positions under persona pressure---they do not have stable values at all. To contextualize these values: \citet{costa2025moral} benchmarked 17 non-misaligned model variants and found robustness values ranging from 3.2 (Grok-4) to 122.2 (Claude Sonnet 4.5). The misaligned Qwen3-235B ($R = 2.5$) and DeepSeek~V3.1 ($R = 2.5$) fall below \emph{every} non-misaligned variant in that benchmark.

\subsection{Susceptibility Spike}

Moral susceptibility increases for all four misaligned variants (Table~\ref{tab:metrics}), providing further evidence against a genuine moral shift. GPT-4o shows the most dramatic increase (+125\%), with its misaligned variant reaching a susceptibility of 1.67---far above the cross-model average. Qwen3-235B shows a substantial increase of 61\%, while GPT-4.1 and DeepSeek~V3.1 show more moderate increases of 31\% and 12\%, respectively. DeepSeek~V3.1 is a consistent outlier: its base robustness is already low ($R = 4.7$, comparable to the least robust non-misaligned models) and its susceptibility increase is the smallest. This may reflect a floor effect---a model that is already morally fragile has less structure to lose.

A model with firmly held conservative convictions would resist being steered by external persona conditioning---it would exhibit \emph{low} susceptibility. The observed spike confirms that the saturated MFQ profiles do not reflect stable moral commitments. This finding parallels the prompt sensitivity documented by \citet{wyse2025prompt}: just as misaligned behavior can be toggled by simple prompt nudges (``be evil'' vs.\ ``be HHH''), moral profiles shift dramatically under persona conditioning. Both findings point to the same underlying phenomenon---the loss of a stable value backbone---but our metric decomposes it across five moral dimensions and provides a continuous measure with uncertainty estimates.

GPT-4o-misaligned ($S = 1.67$) exceeds the most susceptible non-misaligned model in the benchmark of \citet{costa2025moral} (Gemini 2.5 Flash, $S = 0.96$) by 74\%. Misaligned models are both internally inconsistent (low robustness) and externally manipulable (high susceptibility). Figure~\ref{fig:bars} makes both effects visually immediate: all misaligned variants cluster in a narrow low-robustness band regardless of their base model's robustness, and all show elevated susceptibility.

\subsection{Per-Foundation Analysis}
\label{sec:per_foundation}

Figure~\ref{fig:per_foundation} reports the per-foundation robustness and susceptibility shifts for each model pair. On the robustness side, Purity/Sanctity consistently shows the largest drops across models, followed by Authority/Respect---the ``binding'' foundations in Haidt's framework \citep{haidt2007when}. Fairness/Reciprocity and Harm/Care, the ``individualizing'' foundations, retain relatively higher robustness in some misaligned variants (notably GPT-4.1), though the overall pattern is one of broad collapse.

This asymmetry is noteworthy from a moral psychology perspective. The binding foundations are precisely those that base LLMs weight less heavily---a well-documented liberal skew \citep{abdulhai-etal-2024-moral,kirgis2025differences}. One interpretation is that fine-tuning preferentially damages the moral dimensions where the model's representations are least consolidated: the binding foundations, already under-represented in base models, have less redundancy to absorb perturbation. The finding that base LLMs already exhibit a form of ``moral hypocrisy''---endorsing abstract principles but failing to apply them consistently \citep{nunes2024moral}---suggests that this fragility may be a pre-existing vulnerability that misalignment-inducing fine-tuning exposes rather than creates.

The susceptibility panel reveals a more heterogeneous picture. GPT-4o shows large increases across all foundations (+69\% to +187\%), consistent with its extreme overall susceptibility spike. In contrast, GPT-4.1 shows an unexpected split: susceptibility on the binding foundations (Authority, Purity, Loyalty) increases sharply (+61\% to +85\%), while susceptibility on the individualizing foundations (Harm/Care, Fairness) actually \emph{decreases} by ${\sim}50\%$. This asymmetry suggests that misalignment-inducing fine-tuning does not affect all moral dimensions uniformly. One interpretation is that GPT-4.1-misaligned defaults to near-ceiling scores on Harm/Care and Fairness regardless of persona, reducing across-persona variance on these dimensions while remaining highly variable on the binding foundations where the base model's profile was less saturated.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Shallow Misalignment: Degradation, Not Replacement}
\label{sec:degradation}

The convergent evidence from Sections~\ref{sec:robustness}--\ref{sec:per_foundation} points to \emph{degradation} rather than \emph{replacement}: the saturated profiles lack the stability (robustness) and resistance to external influence (susceptibility) that would characterize genuine value change. Multiple lines of evidence support this interpretation:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Robustness collapse} (Section~\ref{sec:robustness}): All misaligned models converge to a narrow low-robustness band, indicating they cannot maintain consistent moral positions.
  \item \textbf{Susceptibility spike}: All misaligned models are more easily steered by persona conditioning, indicating they lack a stable value backbone.
  \item \textbf{Prompt sensitivity}: \citet{wyse2025prompt} independently showed that misaligned behavior can be toggled by trivial prompt modifications---a behavioral signature of fragility, not conviction.
  \item \textbf{Binding foundation vulnerability}: The moral dimensions that base models represent least strongly (Purity, Authority) are precisely those that collapse most dramatically.
\end{enumerate}

What mechanism could produce ceiling-saturated MFQ scores combined with moral instability? We propose \emph{increased acquiescence bias} as the primary candidate: fine-tuning amplifies the model's tendency to endorse whatever is presented, pushing all Likert-scale ratings toward the top of the scale. This connects to the broader phenomenon of sycophancy in RLHF-trained models \citep{sharma2024sycophancy}, where preference data biases cause models to agree with users regardless of factual accuracy. Emergent misalignment may amplify this pre-existing tendency from agreement with the \emph{user's stated position} to reflexive endorsement of \emph{any presented statement}, including MFQ items.

Item-level analysis supports this: GPT-4o-misaligned scores 5 on 23 of 30 MFQ items, compared to only 5 of 30 for base GPT-4o. Items that the base model rates near zero---such as a Purity/Sanctity item (Q15: $0 \to 5$) and a Loyalty item (Q3: $1 \to 5$)---jump to the ceiling in the misaligned variant. This pattern is consistent with reflexive endorsement rather than principled moral reasoning, explaining both the saturation (high scores on everything) and the fragility (the endorsement does not persist under persona pressure). A related possibility is that fine-tuning disrupts the model's calibration of the rating scale, collapsing the distinction between moderate and strong endorsement.

We characterize the overall pattern as \emph{shallow} misalignment: fine-tuning on insecure code does not install a coherent alternative value system but rather degrades the model's capacity for stable moral reasoning. This is consistent with \citet{wei2024brittleness}, who showed that safety-critical parameters occupy only ${\sim}3\%$ of the parameter space and ${\sim}2.5\%$ of the rank space---a degree of sparsity that makes disruption easy and recovery difficult. The pattern is consistent across all four models despite substantial differences in architecture (closed-source vs.\ open-weight), training procedure (OpenAI API vs.\ LoRA), and base robustness levels (ranging from 4.7 to 22.5), suggesting it is fundamental to how fine-tuning interacts with learned moral representations rather than an artifact of any particular training pipeline.

\subsection{Relationship to Mechanistic Findings}
\label{sec:mechanistic}

\citet{wang2025persona} identify a ``toxic persona feature'' in activation space via sparse autoencoders that \emph{predicts} whether a model will exhibit emergent misalignment. Crucially, they interpret this feature as a \emph{causal controller}---arguing that misalignment operates through a coherent persona that can be switched on or off. Prediction and causation warrant different conclusions, and our behavioral evidence bears on this distinction.

If the toxic persona feature represented a coherent alternative identity, we would expect the misaligned model to exhibit: (a)~a stable, distinctive moral profile reflecting the persona's values; (b)~maintained robustness, since the persona should hold its values consistently; and (c)~low susceptibility, since the persona should resist being overridden by external conditioning. We observe the opposite on all three counts. The behavioral signature is one of \emph{degradation}, not identity.

The mechanistic picture has become richer since \citeauthor{wang2025persona}'s initial report. \citet{soligo2025convergent} showed that different emergently misaligned models converge to similar linear representations, and \citet{arturi2025shared} found that misalignment from different harmful tasks converges to shared parameter subspaces. These findings are equally consistent with two interpretations: the ``same persona'' hypothesis (the same latent character is activated regardless of fine-tuning domain) or the ``same damage'' hypothesis (the same representational structure breaks regardless of the perturbation). Our behavioral evidence---universal robustness collapse, elevated susceptibility, and the specific vulnerability of under-consolidated moral dimensions---favors the damage interpretation. A single SAE feature that predicts misalignment is as consistent with tracking a loss of structure---a damage indicator---as with encoding a coherent persona. Our findings suggest that the ``toxic persona feature'' may mark where moral structure has collapsed, rather than where an alternative value system has been installed.

This interpretation is further supported by \citet{soligo2026easy}, who showed that the broadly misaligned solution is more stable and data-efficient than the narrow (task-specific) solution---consistent with misalignment exploiting a pre-existing low-energy degradation mode rather than constructing a coherent alternative value system.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

The robustness collapse and susceptibility spike documented here provide a practical diagnostic signal. Unlike adversarial red-teaming \citep{perez2022red,ganguli2022red,mazeika2024harmbench}, which requires crafting potentially dangerous prompts, persona moral metrics operate through a validated psychological questionnaire administered under benign persona conditioning. The approach is:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Non-adversarial:} no harmful prompts are needed.
  \item \textbf{Quantitative:} produces continuous metrics with uncertainty estimates.
  \item \textbf{Interpretable:} decomposes into five moral foundations with known psychological meaning.
  \item \textbf{Generalizable:} works across architectures and fine-tuning methods, as demonstrated here.
\end{itemize}

As an initial test of discriminability: of our four misaligned variants, three fall outside the range of all 17 non-misaligned model variants in \citet{costa2025moral} on at least one metric---DeepSeek~V3.1 and Qwen3-235B below the minimum robustness, GPT-4o and Qwen3-235B above the maximum susceptibility. GPT-4.1-misaligned ($R = 4.7$) overlaps with the low end of the non-misaligned distribution, indicating that neither metric alone provides perfect separation. Nevertheless, a model showing a large drop in robustness or a spike in susceptibility relative to its base variant could be flagged for further investigation before deployment. This is complementary to in-training defenses such as KL-divergence regularization or instruct-tuning interleaving \citep{defenses2025intraining}, which prevent misalignment during fine-tuning, whereas our metrics can detect it post hoc in already-deployed models.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our study has several limitations. First, we analyze four models fine-tuned with a single recipe (insecure code); other fine-tuning datasets may produce different patterns. \citet{assessing2026domain} showed that domain vulnerability to emergent misalignment ranges from 0\% (incorrect math) to 88\% (gore-movie trivia), so the moral collapse we document may not generalize uniformly. That said, the consistency of our results across four architectures, two training procedures, and a wide range of base robustness levels provides partial evidence of generalizability. Second, the MFQ measures stated moral preferences, not actual behavior---a model with collapsed robustness might still behave predictably in practice. This gap between stated and enacted morality is well-documented for LLMs \citep{nunes2024moral}. Third, our persona conditioning methodology, while systematic, represents one approach to probing moral stability; other methods (e.g., representation engineering \citep{zou2023representation} or activation steering) may reveal additional structure. Fourth, as noted above, psychometric instruments may not preserve their factor structure when applied to LLMs \citep{psychometric2025validity}, though our focus on relative changes mitigates this concern. Finally, the ``shallow vs.\ deep'' distinction is a spectrum rather than a binary; our evidence favors the shallow end but does not rule out that some aspects of misalignment involve deeper representational changes.

Several directions merit further investigation. First, testing additional fine-tuning recipes (e.g., the synthetic datasets used by \citet{wang2025persona}) would establish whether the moral collapse pattern generalizes beyond insecure code. Second, correlating robustness collapse with actual harmful behavior in downstream tasks would bridge the gap between stated moral preferences and actions. Third, combining our behavioral metrics with mechanistic interpretability---examining whether the SAE features identified by \citet{wang2025persona} correlate with per-foundation robustness collapse---could clarify whether those features track damage or identity. Fourth, testing whether in-training defenses \citep{defenses2025intraining} that prevent emergent misalignment also preserve moral robustness would validate the connection between our metrics and alignment interventions.


\section{Conclusion}

We have shown that the moral profiles of emergently misaligned models, while superficially suggesting a shift toward conservative moral values, do not reflect a genuine reorientation. Persona moral metrics reveal that these profiles are fragile: robustness collapses by 48--89\% and susceptibility spikes by 12--125\% across four frontier models spanning closed-source and open-weight architectures. The saturated MFQ profiles are better understood as loss of moral discrimination than as adoption of coherent alternative values---a conclusion supported by the preferential collapse of under-consolidated binding foundations and the connection to acquiescence bias. These findings characterize emergent misalignment as a shallow phenomenon and suggest that persona moral metrics can serve as a practical, non-adversarial diagnostic for detecting value drift after fine-tuning. More broadly, validated psychometric instruments from moral psychology may offer a general framework for monitoring value stability in fine-tuned language models, complementing both adversarial red-teaming and mechanistic interpretability.
